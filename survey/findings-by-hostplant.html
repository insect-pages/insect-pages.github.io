Explanation of records table

Top

The records table provides the full list of insect-plant associations I observed in the survey, with each association between a stem-feeding insect and a host plant genus given its own row in the table.

Breaking down a record, column by column

The digits in the first column at the start of each row form an identifier, unique to each record, which I designated to help streamline recordkeeping. After the record number, the host genus, host family, and two-letter abbreviations for the observed host species are given. The two-letter host species abbreviations are expanded in the Hostplant species codes section, below.

The next column in the table specifies the feeding mode I have observed for the insect (as defined in the Introduction).

After the feeding mode, I list the taxonomic information for the insect, if known. In many cases an insect has not yet been identified to species, but appears similar to a known taxon, based on my observations of its life stages in comparison with published information about the known taxon. In these cases I have used "cf." to indicate the insect should be compared to the known taxon.

In the insect taxonomy column, I also provide a footnote link (e.g., "[1]") if I observed the insect in an Upper Midwest U.S. state other than Iowa, in which case the U.S. state(s) are given in the corresponding footnote number at the bottom of the table. All records were observed in Iowa only, unless such a footnote link is given.

In the next column, the following abbreviations are used in square brackets to indicate the life stages I have observed for each insect record.

  • T: Trace; tunnel, mine, gall, or other damage to plant / feeding sign
  • E: Egg / Oviposition area in plant
  • L: Larva
  • P: Pupa / Puparium
  • A: Adult (reared, or in rare cases, observed ovipositing in the wild)

Not all records have their own dedicated report page yet. For those that do, a link is given in the final column at the end of the row. Some of these reports have both text and photos; others, just text so far, but with photos hopefully added soon, once I am able to prepare internet-ready versions from my photo library.

Use the list of hostplant genera to navigate to the entries for a particular hostplant genus in the table.

Alternatively, you can type the hashtag character (#) at the end of the URL in your browser's address bar, followed by the genus name in lowercase, and then press Enter/Return. For example, adding "#ageratina" without the quotes to the URL in the address bar and then pressing Enter/Return will take you to the Ageratina entries in the table.

To navigate to an individual record in the table, append "#r" followed by the four-digit record number to the URL in your browser's address bar (e.g., "#r0010" without the quotes) and press Enter/Return.
